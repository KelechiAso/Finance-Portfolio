My Guide to DCF Analysis

I've learned that **DCF** (Discounted Cash Flow) analysis is one of the most powerful valuation methods for determining a company's intrinsic value. The core idea is simple but profound: a company's value today is the sum of all the cash it is expected to generate in the future, with each of those future cash flows "discounted" by a rate that reflects the time value of money and risk.

What is DCF?

The goal of a DCF is to figure out what a company is truly worth, independent of market speculation. This is achieved by forecasting the company's future **Free Cash Flow to Firm (FCFF)**, which is the cash available to all of its capital providers (both debt and equity holders). The process involves two main components:

1. Forecasting Period

I start by projecting the company's FCFF for a specific period, typically 5 to 10 years. This requires making detailed assumptions about revenue growth, operating expenses, capital expenditures (CapEx), and changes in working capital. The FCFF is calculated like this:


FCFF = EBIT * (1 - Tax Rate) + Depreciation & Amortization - CapEx - Change in Net Working Capital

After forecasting, all of these projected cash flows are then discounted back to their present value.

2. Terminal Value

Since I can't forecast a company's cash flows forever, I calculate a **Terminal Value (TV)**. This represents the value of the company at the end of my forecasting period and beyond. It can be calculated in two ways:

  • Perpetuity Growth Model: This assumes the company's cash flows will grow at a stable, perpetual rate forever. I use the following formula, with a conservative perpetuity growth rate (g).
    • 
Terminal Value = (FCFF_YearN * (1 + g)) / (WACC - g)
  • Exit Multiple Method: This applies a valuation multiple (like EV/EBITDA) to the final year's EBITDA to determine the terminal value.

How DCF is Used in Finance

I find DCF to be central to many strategic and investment decisions:

  • Investment Decisions: I use DCF to determine if an investment is worthwhile. If the intrinsic value I calculate is higher than the current market price, the investment is considered attractive.
  • Mergers and Acquisitions (M&A): DCF is a critical tool for determining a fair price for a target company. I'll use a DCF model to analyze the cash flows I expect to receive from the acquisition.
  • Capital Budgeting: Companies use DCF to evaluate the viability of new projects. The expected cash flows from a new project are discounted to see if it creates value for the firm.

Example Calculation

I'll continue with "Tech Innovators Inc." and do a simplified DCF analysis. My assumptions are based on my WACC from a previous guide:


Assumptions:
Forecast Period: 5 years
WACC: 8.21%
FCFF Projections:
Year 1: $30 million
Year 2: $35 million
Year 3: $40 million
Year 4: $45 million
Year 5: $50 million
Perpetuity Growth Rate (g): 2.5%

Step 1: Discount the Forecasted Cash Flows

I use the WACC as my discount rate to find the present value of each cash flow. I'll sum them up to get the total PV.


PV of FCFF = 
(30M / (1+0.0821)^1) + (35M / (1+0.0821)^2) + ...
Sum of Discounted Cash Flows = $155.93M

Step 2: Calculate the Terminal Value (TV)

I'll use the Perpetuity Growth Model to calculate the value of the company after year 5.


TV at Year 5 = (FCFF_Year6) / (WACC - g)
TV at Year 5 = (50M * (1+0.025)) / (0.0821 - 0.025) = $897.55M

I then have to discount this Terminal Value back to its present value, which gives me a PV of TV of $602.82M.

Step 3: Calculate the Final Enterprise Value

The final Enterprise Value is the sum of the PV of the forecasted cash flows and the PV of the terminal value. 


Enterprise Value = $155.93M + $602.82M = $758.75M

To get the value of equity, I subtract the company's net debt ($200M).


Equity Value = $758.75M - $200M = $558.75M

Key Limitations of DCF

While DCF is a robust method, its reliance on assumptions makes it susceptible to error. The main limitations I've learned about are:

  • Sensitivity to Assumptions: A DCF valuation can change drastically with small changes in the perpetuity growth rate or the WACC. I always perform a sensitivity analysis to see a range of possible outcomes.
  • "Garbage In, Garbage Out": The accuracy of the DCF is entirely dependent on the quality of my financial projections. If my assumptions are flawed, the final valuation will be flawed.
  • The Terminal Value Problem: The terminal value often accounts for 60-80% of a DCF's final value, which means the model is highly reliant on my assumptions about what happens far into the future.
